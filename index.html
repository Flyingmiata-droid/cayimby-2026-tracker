Priority 1 — AB 1621 — Housing Accountability Act / postentitlement permits

AB 1621 SUPPORT

Authors: Wilson, Blanca Rubio, Wicks · Source: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov

Status: In Senate · referred to Local Government and Housing committees on 2026-05-06 · Senate committee hearing date TBD

What it does

Closes a long-running loophole in the Housing Accountability Act by extending its protections to postentitlement-phase permits — the building permits, grading permits, and utility connections that cities issue after a project is already entitled. Today, cities that can't legally block a project under HAA at the entitlement stage routinely delay or deny postentitlement permits as a backdoor veto. AB 1621 brings those decisions under HAA's "objective standards only" framework and the same five-and-a-half-times damages remedy.

What changed this week

Read third time in the Assembly and passed 67-0 with no opposition on 2026-04-27. Referred to Senate Rules on 2026-04-28, then assigned to Senate Local Government and Senate Housing on 2026-05-06. The Senate Housing committee chair has not yet set a hearing date — letters of support sent before the hearing is calendared land in the analyst's first cut.

Where you can move the needle

  • Senate Housing Committee analyst — letter of support from Cal YIMBY citing the postentitlement-delay pattern across at least 3 documented projects in CA YIMBY's litigation tracker will frame the analyst's writeup.
  • Coalition letter — Wilson, Rubio, and Wicks together signal broad Assembly Dem support; a coalition letter to Senate Housing co-signed by Cal YIMBY + YIMBY Action + Abundant Housing LA (and ideally the building trades) gives the bill an "everyone's-on-board" frame for any Senator weighing committee opposition.
  • Member action — the bill passed Assembly with zero opposition, so the lift in Senate is procedural, not persuasion. The constituent-email moment is when the hearing gets calendared — likely 2-3 weeks out.

Counter-advocacy to expect

League of California Cities will run "local control" framing. Plumas County and a few other rural jurisdictions filed boilerplate letters against AB 130 / SB 35 with the same language — likely repeat here. Standard rebuttals: HAA already applies to entitlement, this just closes the workaround; postentitlement delays have already produced three published court decisions documenting the abuse pattern; objective-standards review preserves all valid local authority.

Draft committee testimony (Senate Housing — ~2 min reading time)

Chair {{chair_name}} and Members of the Committee,

I'm {{name}} with California YIMBY in support of AB 1621.

The Housing Accountability Act is the strongest tool California has given housing applicants to challenge unlawful local denials. It works — when it applies. The problem this bill fixes is that HAA stops at the entitlement decision. After approval, cities that lost the legal fight at the planning commission get a second chance to kill the project through delay: refusing to issue grading permits for six months, demanding redesign of approved plans during the building-permit review, withholding utility connection sign-offs without articulable cause.

Three published Court of Appeal decisions in the last 18 months document this pattern. In each, the project was lawfully approved. In each, the city's postentitlement delay killed the financing or the construction window. In each, the developer had no HAA remedy because the statute did not reach those decisions.

AB 1621 closes that gap with three changes. First, it brings postentitlement-phase permits explicitly within HAA's "objective standards only" rule, so a city cannot impose a new subjective requirement after the project is entitled. Second, it applies HAA's existing damages remedy — including the five-and-a-half-times multiplier — to bad-faith postentitlement denials. Third, it preserves every legitimate ministerial review function cities perform after entitlement; nothing in the bill prevents cities from enforcing the building code or requiring an inspection.

AB 1621 passed this body's counterpart 67 to 0 with no opposition. It is the natural completion of the HAA framework this Legislature has built over the last decade. Cal YIMBY respectfully asks for an "aye" vote.

Thank you.

Priority 2 — SB 1116 — Planning and zoning: housing development projects: subdivisions

SB 1116 SUPPORT

Author: Caballero · Source: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov

Status: On Senate Appropriations suspense file as of 2026-05-11 · suspense hearing within 2-3 weeks

What it does

Extends the housing-supply procedural protections that currently apply only to entire housing development projects to subdivision applications that propose housing. In practice, this means a developer who breaks ground in three phases — common for missing-middle infill — gets the same shot-clock, ministerial-review, and CEQA-streamlining benefits at each phase as a single-phase project. Reduces the "first phase approved, second phase blocked" trap.

What changed this week

Placed on the Senate Appropriations suspense file on 2026-05-11 after a brief hearing — the standard parking spot for bills with any state fiscal impact. Suspense hearings are usually in the second-to-last week of the committee window, when many bills die without a public vote. Author work and stakeholder pressure between now and the suspense hearing is what determines which side of that line this bill lands on.

Where you can move the needle

  • Senate Appropriations chair and vice chair — short letter framing fiscal impact as cost-saving (fewer second-phase approvals = less staff time on duplicative reviews).
  • Author's office (Caballero) — ask what amendments would unstick it from suspense. Often a small fiscal carveout opens the door.

Counter-advocacy to expect

Cities that have used phased-approval gamesmanship to block missing-middle projects will frame this as "ministerial creep." The fiscal argument cuts both ways — state lege analysts price duplicate review as a cost, not a benefit.

Draft talking points for member calls (use as-is or trim)

If asked: "Why does this matter if HAA already exists?"
→ HAA protects the project. This bill protects each phase of the project.
  Missing-middle infill almost always phases. The current law lets cities
  approve phase 1, then change the standards before phase 2 — and there's
  no remedy because each phase is technically a new application.

If asked: "Will this drive sprawl or coastal-zone development?"
→ No. The bill applies the same exemptions HAA already uses — coastal
  zone, very-high-fire-hazard zones, agricultural easements all carved out.

If asked: "Why is it on suspense?"
→ Appropriations parks anything with a fiscal note. CalHCD will need to
  update guidance, and that's the cost the analyst is pricing. We're
  asking the committee to weigh that against the cost of duplicative
  approvals already being borne by applicants and cities.

Priority 3 — AB 2480 — Density bonus for student housing

AB 2480 SUPPORT

Authors: Ávila Farías, Ransom · Source: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov

Status: Read second time, ordered to Assembly third reading as of 2026-05-07. Floor vote next.

What it does

Extends Density Bonus Law eligibility to dedicated student housing developments, treating bed-count rather than unit-count for the affordability and density-bonus calculations. Resolves the long-standing mismatch where 200-bed student projects didn't qualify for the same density bonus a 50-unit apartment building would.

What changed this week

Cleared Assembly Appropriations 14-0 on 2026-05-06 with no opposition and was ordered to third reading the next day. Will hit the Assembly floor in the next 2-3 session days. Given the unanimous committee vote, floor passage looks routine — flag it more for newsletter coverage than urgent member action.
